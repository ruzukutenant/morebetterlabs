"Students will feel more confident." "Learners will understand the fundamentals of marketing." "Participants will gain valuable skills."

These are outcomes from real course descriptions. They share a critical flaw: they're unmeasurable. How confident? Understand how deeply? Which skills, specifically? If you can't tell whether your students achieved the outcome, you can't design a course that reliably produces it.

Defining measurable outcomes isn't just good practice — it's the foundation that every other design decision rests on. Your course structure, content, activities, and assessments should all trace back to a specific, verifiable result. Without that anchor, you're building a course around a feeling rather than a destination.

## Start With the End: What Lasting Change Are You Creating?

Most course creators start by listing what they want to teach. This is backwards. Start with what the student will be different *after*.

### The Dream Exercise

Here's an exercise I use in my World Class Courses workshop:

> "Imagine that you have a group of dream students. They are engaged, they are perfectly behaved, and they have perfect memories. Fill in this sentence: **3-5 years from now, my students still know... Or they still are able to do... Or they still find value in...**"

The 3-5 year timeframe is deliberate. It forces you to identify *durable* outcomes — not what students will remember from Module 3 next week, but what will still be shaping their practice years later.

This exercise shifts your thinking from "what content do I need to deliver?" to "what lasting change am I creating?" A course on social media marketing might produce the transient outcome "students know the current Instagram algorithm." The durable outcome is "students can analyze any platform's algorithm and adapt their strategy" — a skill that lasts regardless of which platform is trending.

## Three Dimensions of Success: Know, Feel, Do

Once you've identified the lasting change, define it across three dimensions. This framework, adapted from Yale's instructional design tradition, captures the full scope of what learning can produce:

- **Know**: What will your students understand? What knowledge will they retain?
- **Feel**: What emotional state will they achieve? Confidence? Clarity? Calm? Motivation?
- **Do**: What will they be able to *do* that they couldn't do before?

Most course creators focus almost entirely on "Know." But the research on adult learning (Knowles, 1980) makes clear that adults are primarily motivated by internal factors — self-esteem, quality of life, personal satisfaction. These map directly to "Feel" and "Do."

**Example — a course on public speaking:**
- **Know**: The structure of an effective 10-minute presentation; how to handle Q&A
- **Feel**: Confidence stepping up to speak; calm rather than panicked before a presentation
- **Do**: Deliver a polished presentation to a live audience; handle unexpected questions without freezing

Notice how the "Feel" and "Do" dimensions change the course design. A Know-only course is a set of lectures about presentation technique. A Know-Feel-Do course includes practice presentations, feedback from peers, progressive desensitization to speaking anxiety, and a final live delivery. The same topic, designed completely differently, producing fundamentally different outcomes.

## Making Outcomes Measurable

Now comes the rigorous step: "How will we know whether our students have achieved the outcomes — or not?"

The criteria don't need to be numeric. They need to be *observable* — you can see, read, or verify that the student did it.

| Course Type | Vague Outcome | Measurable Criterion |
|-------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Stress reduction | "Feel less stressed" | Self-assessment score improves within 8 weeks |
| Novel writing | "Learn to write fiction" | At least 5,000 words written + outline for the rest of the book |
| Facebook ads | "Understand digital marketing" | 10% improvement in leads from ads |
| Coaching certification | "Become a better coach" | Successfully conduct 3 supervised coaching sessions with written client feedback |
| Course creation | "Know how to build a course" | Publish a complete pilot course with at least 5 enrolled students |

The left column is what most course descriptions say. The right column is what you can actually design toward — and what students can actually achieve.

This approach aligns with Wiggins and McTighe's Understanding by Design framework (2005): start with the evidence of learning (how will you know they got it?), then design the experiences that produce that evidence. Not content first, then outcomes as an afterthought.

The connection to [the two traps of course design](/learning-design/the-two-traps-that-kill-online-courses/) is direct: the Aboutness Trap produces courses organized around topics with no clear outcomes. Measurable criteria force you out of the trap because you can't be vague about what "about marketing" actually means for the student.

## Four Questions to Right-Size Your Outcome

Even with clear outcomes, most course creators scope too broadly. "Master digital marketing" is measurable in theory but would take years. You need outcomes that are achievable within your course's timeframe.

These four scoping questions, which I teach in Mirasee's course design program, progressively tighten the scope:

**1. "How long will my course need to be to deliver on this promise?"**

Most pilots shouldn't be more than four to six weeks. If you need much longer, you're probably trying to deliver too much. Scale back to the outcome that's achievable in that timeframe.

**2. "If someone started my course but quit after a month, would they see any benefits?"**

If so, what would those benefits be? How could you concentrate the most possible value into those first four weeks? Can you make *those* the only outcomes of the course?

**3. "What would be the first big milestone that gives students a real visceral sense that this is working?"**

If that milestone is compelling enough on its own, can you make it the entire promise of the course? A course that reliably delivers one clear milestone is more valuable than a course that promises five and delivers none.

**4. "If you were on a two-hour plane ride with your ideal student, what could you teach them that would leave them saying, 'Wow, that really helped me'?"**

Can you limit the scope of your course to what you'd teach in those two hours?

Each question tightens the frame: 4-6 weeks → first month → first milestone → two hours. The plane ride question is especially powerful because it bypasses the expert's instinct to be comprehensive. Two hours strips away everything except the highest-impact content.

## The Process in Practice

Putting it all together:

1. **Dream Exercise**: What will students still know, do, or value in 3-5 years?
2. **Know-Feel-Do**: Define success across all three dimensions
3. **Measurable Criteria**: How will you verify the outcome was achieved?
4. **Scoping Questions**: Right-size the outcome for your course format
5. **Design backward**: From the verified outcome, work backward to the structure, activities, and content that produce it

This process takes real thinking — often more thinking than the course creation itself. But it's the thinking that separates courses that transform from courses that merely inform. Every hour spent clarifying your outcome saves ten hours of building content that doesn't serve anyone.
