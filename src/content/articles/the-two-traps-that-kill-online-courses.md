After a decade of building [Ruzuku](https://www.ruzuku.com) and watching thousands of courses succeed or fail, I've seen the same two design failures kill courses over and over. Not bad marketing. Not wrong pricing. Bad learning design.

I call them the two traps — and nearly every struggling course creator is stuck in at least one.

## Trap 1: The Instructionism Trap

The Instructionism Trap is the default mode of course creation: you know something, so you tell people about it. You record lectures. You create slide decks. You write long explanations. The student's job is to watch, read, and absorb.

This feels natural because it mirrors how most of us experienced school. A teacher stands at the front and talks. Students sit and listen. Knowledge is "transmitted" from expert to novice.

The problem is that this model of learning — called instructionism — doesn't match how people actually learn. Freeman et al.'s landmark 2014 meta-analysis in *PNAS*, examining 225 studies, found that students in active learning environments were 1.5 times less likely to fail compared to lecture-based instruction. The average exam scores increased by half a letter grade. Telling isn't teaching.

### The Curse of Knowledge

The Instructionism Trap is powered by a cognitive bias: the Curse of Knowledge. Once you know something, it's genuinely hard to remember what it was like not to know it. You organize information in ways that make sense *to an expert* — but not to a beginner.

This is related to what Kalyuga et al. (2003) call the expertise reversal effect: instructional methods that help novices can actually hinder experts, and vice versa. When an expert designs a course, they instinctively structure it the way they think about the material — which is often exactly wrong for someone encountering it for the first time.

The Curse of Knowledge manifests in specific ways:
- **Skipping "obvious" steps** that aren't obvious to beginners
- **Using jargon** without realizing it's jargon
- **Organizing by conceptual categories** that only make sense once you already understand the field
- **Moving too fast** through foundations because they feel boring to the expert

In my seminars at UNC's School of Education, I see this consistently: experienced practitioners who are brilliant at their craft struggle to break their expertise into learnable steps. The expertise that makes them great practitioners is the same expertise that makes course design unintuitive.

### How to Escape Trap 1

Design for *doing*, not for *knowing*. Every piece of content in your course should connect to an action the student takes. Not "watch this video about pricing strategies" but "apply this pricing framework to your course and share your result."

This doesn't mean eliminating content — it means subordinating content to activity. Content exists to support the doing, not the other way around. My research on designing context-aware capture systems for education (Crystal, 2006) explored this principle directly: educational technology should support learning *in context*, not deliver content in isolation.

## Trap 2: The Aboutness Trap

The Aboutness Trap is subtler. You've designed a course "about" a topic — about marketing, about meditation, about course creation. It's organized by subject matter. Module 1: Introduction to X. Module 2: Fundamentals of Y. Module 3: Advanced Z.

The problem: creating a course "about" a topic is fundamentally different from creating a course that solves a problem or helps someone improve their performance, results, or experiences. "About" courses are organized around the *subject's* structure. Effective courses are organized around the *learner's* journey.

The Aboutness Trap produces two specific symptoms:

### Incoherent Organization and Sequencing

When you organize by topic, you create a taxonomy — a logical structure that mirrors how an expert thinks about the field. But students don't need a taxonomy. They need a path.

In my research on information architecture (Crystal, 2007), I studied how the structure of information fundamentally changes how people can navigate and use it. The same content, organized differently, produces different outcomes. Topic-based organization makes sense to the person who created it. Path-based organization — "here's what you need this week to take your next step" — makes sense to the person learning it.

### "Overdosing"

The second symptom is what course creator Jen Louden calls "overdosing" — cramming too much content and too many activities into a course without regard for cognitive load. This happens because Aboutness Trap thinking leads to comprehensiveness: if the course is "about" a topic, you need to cover all of it.

Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988) explains why this fails. Working memory has strict limits — roughly 4-7 items at a time. When a course overloads working memory with too much new information, learning doesn't just slow down. It stops. Students feel overwhelmed, fall behind, and drop out.

Overdosing isn't about having too much content in total. It's about presenting too much at once, without spacing, without practice, without the cognitive breathing room that lets new information consolidate into long-term memory.

## The Escape: Outcome-Focused Design with Dual Minimalism

Both traps share a root cause: starting with what you want to *teach* instead of what the student needs to *achieve*. The escape is what I call **Dual Minimalism**:

1. **Focused, well-scoped outcomes that deliver value** — not "learn about marketing" but "get your first 10 students enrolled"
2. **Minimal content to scaffold progress toward those outcomes** — only include what's needed to get there

This isn't about making courses short or cheap. It's about ruthless clarity on what matters. Every piece of content earns its place by directly supporting the student's progress toward the outcome. Everything else gets cut — not because it's bad content, but because it's a distraction.

The starting question: "Based on everything I've learned from my research and talking to customers, **what do people really want from my course?**" Not what topic do they want to learn about. What result do they want to achieve? What problem do they want solved? What experience do they want to have?

Then: **what is the minimum content needed to scaffold their progress toward that result?**

This approach aligns with Wiggins and McTighe's Understanding by Design framework (2005) — start with the desired outcome, then work backward to determine what's actually needed. It also respects Cognitive Load Theory by keeping the information density appropriate to what students can actually process.

## Diagnosing Your Course

If your course is struggling, run this diagnostic:

**Signs you're in the Instructionism Trap:**
- Most of your course is video lectures or text content
- Students' primary activity is watching, reading, or listening
- There are few or no exercises where students apply concepts to their own situation
- Student questions are mostly clarifying questions ("what did you mean by...?") rather than application questions ("I tried this and...")

**Signs you're in the Aboutness Trap:**
- Your course outline reads like a textbook table of contents
- You can't state the specific, measurable outcome in one sentence
- Students say "this was really interesting" but don't change their behavior
- You keep adding content because the course doesn't feel "complete"
- Students feel overwhelmed or fall behind

**Signs you've escaped both:**
- Every lesson has a concrete activity where students do something real
- Your course is organized around a learning path, not a topic hierarchy
- Students produce tangible artifacts (plans, drafts, analyses, frameworks applied to their situation)
- Students achieve observable outcomes — and you know how to verify it

## The Broader Pattern

The two traps aren't just common mistakes. They're the predictable result of designing from expertise rather than designing from research. Experts default to instructionism because that's what they experienced in school. They default to aboutness because that's how they organize their own knowledge.

Escaping requires a deliberate shift: from "what do I know?" to "what does my student need?" From "what should I teach?" to "what should they be able to *do*?"

This shift is what learning design — as a discipline, not just a buzzword — is fundamentally about. And it's what separates courses that merely inform from courses that actually transform.
